Scientific report PN-II-ID-PCE-2011-3-0484

Exploring Robot Assisted Therapy for Children with ASD.

By Prof Dr ir Bram Vanderborght

Performed studies

1. Children with Autism Spectrum Disorders in Joint Attention tasks with a robotic partner

The aim of this exploratory study is to investigate the possibilities of social robots in Joint

Attention interventions for young children with Autism Spectrum Disorders. A repeated

measurement design was used for a sample of 25 children with Autism Spectrum Disorders

between 36 and 52 months old. Each participant performed two Joint Attention tasks, focused on

responding to and initiating Joint Attention, with a robotic and a human partner. Outcomes

showed that participants achieved significantly higher performances with the robot compared to

the adult in both Joint Attention tasks. These results were in contradiction with the existing

research, stating that children with Autism Spectrum Disorders would prefer interacting with

robots over humans. Another object of interest were the characteristics of Autism Spectrum

Disorders that are predictive for the performances with the social robot. According to the results

of the study, the degree of impairment in social skills and the level of functioning might have a

predictive role in the performances of children with Autism Spectrum Disorders with the social

robot Nao. Lastly, the strengths and limitations of the study are presented, as well as directions

for future research.

2. Robot-Enhanced CBT for dysfunctional emotions in social situations for children with ASD

Children with autism have considerable difficulties in identifying and conceptualizing

thoughts and feelings, which affect their ability to manage emotions (Sofronoff, et al., 2007). By

using robot-enhanced therapy (RET) we have tried to: improve the strategies they use in

different social situations; modify irrational beliefs (based on Albert Ellis' REBT/CBT model);

teach them adaptive behaviors in social situations associated with anger and sadness and to

reduce the intensity of negative emotions. 27 children with a primary diagnosis primary

diagnosis of ASD, aged 6 - 12 years, were randomly assigned to either RET (12 children) or

treatment as usual (TAU) group (15 children). Children in the RET group participated in six 2-h

weekly group sessions. Children with ASD from RET group showed statistically significant

more rational beliefs and they had a lower level of emotional responses intensity, after treatment

compared with TAU group. We did not found any significant differences between the two

groups regarding: social knowledge and adaptive behaviors. Our findings showed also important

gains from pre- to posttreatment for children with ASD that participated in the RET group. Our

results may have important clinical implications for improving standard CBT for reducing

emotional distress in children with ASD.

3. Do infants perceive the social robot Keepon as a communicative partner? Infant Behavior and

Development

This study investigates if infants perceive an unfamiliar agent, such as the robot Keepon, as a

social agent after observing an interaction between the robot and a human adult. 23 infants, aged

9-17 month, were exposed, in a first phase, to either a contingent (interactive human adult- interactive robot), or a noncontingent (interactive human adult-non-active robot) interaction

4. The development of a scale measuring ethical acceptability of Robot Enhanced Therapy for

children with Autism Spectrum Disorders.

In order to render the research on the human-robot interaction domain responsive to the

needs and concerns within the general public, the caregivers and therapy communities, we

developed a survey measuring the ethical acceptability of social robots in robot enhanced therapy

(RET) for children with autism spectrum disorder (ASD). The Principal Component Analysis

revealed three components of ethical acceptability: for use, for human-like interaction and for

non-human appearance, each one with a good internal consistency. The results indicate a high

level of ethical acceptability on all the three factors. Men had a higher level of ethical

acceptability of social robots on all the three factors, and younger respondents were more willing

to accept the use of social robots in RET for ASD compared to older ones. Furthermore,

Page 4 of 9

respondents who had previous specific experience with robots had higher levels of ethical

acceptability on all the three factors. Regarding the relation between the involvement with

children with ASD and the ethical acceptability, the data suggest that parents show a good

acceptance level of using social robots in therapy of ASD, but a lower level of acceptance

compared with the group that was not involved with ASD. Education did not influence the level

of ethical acceptability.

On-going studies

1. Are joint attention skills triggered by a social robot? A comparison study with typically

developing children and children with ASD.

A logical step for building a robot assisted therapy on the long term is to investigate if a

child with ASD shows a similar performance with robots compared to humans in tasks where

ASD children are generally failing. There is consensus related to the impairment of ASD

children on joint attention skills, assessed in tasks conducted by human instructors. Robots are

providers of less social complexity and their behaviours can be programmed (i.e. looking to right

and left) and re-adapted to different levels of severity (i.e. the speed of turning the head, adding

small triggers for attiring the attention when moving the eyes, like sounds or other movements

etc.). So, we assume that children with ASD can easier detect and engage in gaze-related

behaviours with a social robot compared to human agents. More than that, the learning RAT

process is relatively easy to implement and hopefully more attractive and motivating for children

with ASD than the traditional approaches (with human therapists). The main aim is to compare

the performances in joint attention tasks of children with ASD with the ones of typically

developing children, when the instructor is a human agent and when the instructor is a social

robot. It will also be examined how this correlation between the performances modifies on

different levels of ASD severity. The second aim will be focused on investigating which are the

characteristics of children with ASD that can be predictive for their performances with the social

robot. 35-40 children in the age range of 4–7 years will be included in the study. Their

performances will be compared with the ones of the typically developing children included in the

2. Social Robots and Autism Spectrum Disorders: A Survey on the Ethical Acceptability and

Perception of Robot Enhanced Therapy for Children with Autism Spectrum Disorders

Research shows that the use of social robots in robot enhanced therapy for children with

autism spectrum disorders has a lot of potential benefits. However, if social robots are to be used

in this type of therapy, it is important to address the ethical issues involved and to know and

understand the underlying attitudes and opinions of the relevant parties that lead to the ethical

acceptance of social robots in robot enhanced therapy for children with autism spectrum

disorders. The aim of the current study was to investigate how individuals with a pervious

interaction with autism spectrum disorders perceive the use of social robots for autism spectrum

disorder treatment and to examine whether certain socio-demographic factors influence this

perception. Participants with different kinds of experience with autism spectrum disorders were

recruited to fill in two questionnaires: the AQ-10 developed by Baron-Cohen and his colleagues

in 2001 to determine the degree of severity of the autism spectrum disorder symptoms, and an

improved version of the ethical acceptability questionnaire developed by Peca et al. (2014) to

measure the participants’ perception of robot enhanced therapy for children with an autism

spectrum disorder. The results of the study suggest an overall positive attitude about robot

enhanced therapy for children with an autism spectrum disorder. People seem to believe in the

potential benefits of using social robots in therapy, and are of the opinion that children with

autism spectrum disorders would enjoy these interactions as well. However, supervision of a

human therapist appears to be a sine qua non for most people, and reservations are expressed

when it comes to matters of safety and trust. Lastly, participants often lacked a defined attitude,

possibly reflecting the novelty of the domain of social robots. Socio-demographic factors seemed

to have little effect on the outcome of the study

3. Measuring social engagement, emotional, cognitive and behavioral skills of children with

autism in a long-term robot-enhanced intervention

The objective of this study was to investigate social engagement, emotional, cognitive

and behavioral reactions of children with ASD during repeated intervention sessions of

interactive games using social robots. This study presents a series of 7 single subject experiments

with ASD children aged between 3 and 5 years. We aimed to investigate if children with autism

show different levels of social engagement, emotions, behaviors and cognitive reactions when

interacting with the Nao robot, compared to a human partner in a imitation task, joint attention

task and turn taking task. The number of intervention sessions for each child for one type of

ability (out of three targeted abilities) was 20 sessions (8 robot enhanced sessions- RET, 8

standard human sessions - SHT and 4 more sessions with the type of intervention gained more

improvements for the child). The Nao robot acts as a social partner in each task, initiating

behaviors like arm movements (in order to be imitated), taking turns and triggering joint

attention episodes. The measured variable were: a. social engagement: eye-contact and verbal

utterances; b. emotional level: positive and negative emotions; c. behavioral level: stereotypical

behaviors, adaptive and maladaptive behaviors; d. cognitive level: rational and irrational beliefs.

Overall, the results of these experiments show mixed results for the efficacy of the RET.

However, most of the time there are no significant differences between the two conditions. A

few differences appeared mostly in the in the imitation task, where children looked more at the

robot compared to the human partner, meaning that the children were interested in the robot

partner during the entire intervention period. Regarding the emotional level, positive emotions

appeared more in the imitation and joint attention task where the robot was the interactional

partner. As for the behavioral level the presence of the robot in the task acts as an behavioral

activator, so that both adaptive and maladaptive behaviors seems to appear more often in RET

condition compared SHT.

1. Simut R.A., Costescu C., Vanderfaeillie J., van de Perre, G., Vanderborght, B., Lefeber, D.,

(in press). "Can you cure me?": Children with autism spectrum disorders playing a doctor game

with a social robot. International Journal of School Health, (in press)

Social robots may represent an important technological instrument for improving children

with ASD social abilities. This work describes an explorative research in which a doctor game

scenario, involving a social robot and a human partner was implemented and tested with children

with autism spectrum disorders (ASD). 30 children diagnosed with ASD between the ages of

four and eight years old were included in this study. The children alternated between playing a

doctor game with the social robot Probo and playing the same game with a human partner, being

exposed to each condition only once in a counter balanced order. The symbolic play task and the

experimental setup was designed to observe whether the children would engage in more

collaborative behaviors while playing the doctor game with a social robot than performing the

same activities with the human partner. Results showed that although the children with ASD

were more interested in and entertained by the robotic partner, the children did not have a better

performance in the game and they did not show significantly more collaborative play and

engagement while playing with the social robot compared with the human partner. Discussion

However, another longer and more thorough study would have to be conducted in order to better

re-interpret these findings and more clearly understand the ASD child- robot interaction. In

conclusion, the obtained findings are discussed within the existing literature and future directions

are proposed.
